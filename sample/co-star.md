# Co-Star — Visual UX Research

Co-Star Personalized Astrology (by Co-Star Astrology Society, iOS-first, ~5M+ users) is the rare consumer app whose **brand voice and visual language are inseparable from its product**. The interface is unapologetically monochrome — pure white, pure black, Helvetica-adjacent neo-grotesk type, and grainy black-and-white collage cutouts of body parts and 17th-century etchings — pitched as "anti-design polish" against the purple/gold mysticism of every other astrology app. But Co-Star's real product is its push notifications: a second-person, mildly hostile, often funny daily ping ("how about you don't expect to be understood today?", "research the pharmaceutical industry") that has become so famous it spawned its own meme genre. Users screenshot and post these notifications constantly — Co-Star treats the notification surface as the primary editorial channel and lets the in-app experience read like an austere reference book rather than a feed. Below is what the screens, copy, and flows actually look like.

## Design language & takeaways

- **Black-on-white absolutism.** No accent color, no shadow, no gradient. The brand palette is literally three values: Black, "Scorpion" dark grey, and "Wild Sand" off-white. The absence reads as confidence, not minimalism-as-cliché.
- **Editorial / book-of-hours typography.** Big neo-grotesk headlines (Helvetica-Now-adjacent), generous leading, copy-heavy screens. The app reads like leafing through a Penguin Classics softcover, not scrolling a feed.
- **Anti-decoration motif: grainy B&W collage.** Where other astrology apps drop in glowing planets and sparkles, Co-Star uses scratchy 17th-century engravings and disembodied body parts (eyes, hands, statues). This is the visual equivalent of a poetry chapbook and signals "this is not for tweens."
- **Notifications are the product.** The app is built so that the daily push *is* the daily horoscope — the in-app screens exist mostly to give the push notification its mythology and context. This is a model worth copying: ship the value to the home screen, let the app be the lore.
- **Parasocial second-person voice.** Push copy is consistently written as if Co-Star is a slightly tired friend who knows you ("how about you don't expect to be understood today?"). The intimacy + the bluntness is what makes screenshots shareable; "vague positive horoscope" copy never goes viral.
- **Personalization friction as a feature.** The onboarding asks for birth city, exact birth time, "do you know your time?" — and presents this gate as proof of seriousness rather than apologizing for it. Compare to wellness apps that hide their precision behind a 3-tap quiz.
- **Social hook is mandatory, not optional.** Friend-add is part of onboarding, not a later tab. Compatibility *is* the retention mechanism — once you have 5 friends in the app, the friction of leaving compounds.
- **Pricing UX matches the brand.** Co-Star Plus paywall avoids the standard "TRY 7 DAYS FREE!" balloon style. It reads like a back-cover blurb. People who buy it are explicitly opting into the editorial tone.
- **Discoverability tradeoffs.** Bottom-nav labels are tiny, icons abstract; designers in published critiques (Pratt, multiple Medium UX case studies) flag that Co-Star sacrifices wayfinding for aesthetic purity. A competitive product can keep the editorial tone while restoring affordances.
- **The "anti-app" positioning is the moat.** Co-Star is visually further from Calm/Headspace/AllTrails than those apps are from each other. For Tend (or any new product) competing on tone-as-product, the lesson is: pick a typographic identity early and refuse to dilute it.
