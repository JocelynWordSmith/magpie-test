# The Cosmic Wheel Sisterhood — Visual Design Research

## Overview

**The Cosmic Wheel Sisterhood** (Deconstructeam / Devolver Digital, 2023) is the single strongest existing game reference for Tend's offering system. The premise: Fortuna, a witch exiled to a floating hut in the cosmic void for 200 years, summons a forbidden entity (Abramar) to forge a new tarot deck — one card at a time, by hand. Each card she creates becomes part of a deck she uses to perform readings for visiting witches; those readings drive branching narrative consequences across the Sisterhood.

The reason this matters for Tend: the **card-creation interface is the closest existing model anywhere in games or apps for a user-built offering / sigil / deity-veve mechanic.** Players don't pick cards from a fixed pool; they *combine* a celestial sphere (suit), a centerpiece icon, decorative motifs, and a thematic frame to forge a unique artifact that is *theirs*. The result feels personal, ritualistic, and irreversible — exactly the emotional register Tend wants for "offerings to a patron deity." It also demonstrates the harder design problem behind that mechanic: a constrained combinatorial system where every output looks intentional and lore-coherent, never like a sticker book.

Beyond the core mechanic, Cosmic Wheel is a masterclass in manga-influenced witch character design, cosmic-void backgrounds, muted purple/gold palettes, and dialogue UI that reads as both diegetic and beautiful. It is the rare occult game that takes paganism, divination, and feminine spirituality seriously without irony or horror gloss — which is also Tend's positioning.

## The tarot card creation interface — the central mechanic

This is the section that matters most for Tend. Cosmic Wheel's card forge is a multi-step constructor: choose a celestial sphere (suit / element), a centerpiece motif, decorative ornaments, and a frame. The UI presents a literal in-world altar where Fortuna physically assembles the card — diegetic, not menu-like.

## Custom card examples — the combinatorial design space

The variety of cards players produce demonstrates that constrained component-systems can yield outputs that feel hand-designed rather than mix-and-match. Even with a fixed component vocabulary, the cards read as singular artifacts because (a) the components are *symbolically* rich, (b) layout is automatically composed for visual coherence, and (c) the palette is locked to a small set of harmonious inks.

## Witch character portraits — Fortuna and visiting witches

Cosmic Wheel's character design is manga-influenced pixel art: large expressive eyes, dramatic hair, witchy fashion (cloaks, jewelry, bold makeup, occasionally horns or magical mutations), and a diverse cast in body type, age, race, and gender presentation. This is a deliberate counter to both "cute pastel witch" and "dark gothic witch" tropes — the witches here are *adults with histories.*

## Divination reading flow & branching consequences

The reading flow is where created cards do work: Fortuna draws from her hand-forged deck, lays cards in a spread, and interprets them for the visiting witch. Interpretations are not pre-scripted to card identity alone — they depend on which cards the player has built. This is the branching consequence layer.

## Color palette

Cosmic Wheel's palette is tightly controlled across all surfaces:

- **Deep purples** (#2A1B3D, #4A2C5F, indigo-violet) as base "void" and night
- **Muted golds and ochres** (#C9A961, #A88746) for accents, frames, candlelight, and "consecrated" highlights
- **Deep forest / olive greens** for organic / herbal elements
- **Dusty rose and burgundy** for flesh, fabric, and warm interior lighting
- **Cosmic gradients** (purple to magenta to teal) for sky / void backdrops
- **Cream / parchment** (#E8D9B5) for readable text surfaces — never pure white

Saturation is deliberately knocked down. Even the brightest accents feel slightly faded, as if seen through candlelight or pigment that has aged. Tend should resist the temptation to push saturation up for "engagement" — the muted-but-rich treatment is core to the perceived sincerity.

## Typography

Two systems:

1. **Display / titles / card names:** ornate humanist serif with celestial flourishes — feels engraved, slightly art-nouveau. Used sparingly.
2. **Body / dialogue:** a soft, slightly rounded sans or low-contrast humanist serif optimized for readability against parchment surfaces.

Card labels themselves are often hand-lettered styling rather than set type, reinforcing that the cards are artifacts, not products.

## Manga-influenced pixel art

The art style is high-resolution pixel art that owes more to '90s shoujo manga and Studio Ghibli than to retro-game pastiche. Faces are detailed and expressive; hair has movement; eyes carry emotion. This is an underexplored aesthetic register for habit apps, which default either to flat vector (Headspace) or polished 3D (Fabulous). A manga-influenced pixel or pixel-adjacent illustration system would let Tend's deity portraits carry real personality and emotional range.

## Witch fashion

Costume design across the visiting witches is a small encyclopedia of witch-fashion options Tend can mine: layered robes with asymmetric drapery, statement jewelry (large pendants, ear cuffs, multi-band rings), bold makeup (graphic eye shadow, dark lips), occasional magical body modifications (horns, glowing marks, unusual eye colors, plant-growth), accessories tied to practice (pouches, daggers, scrying tools, herbs in hair). The vocabulary feels lived-in and varied — these are working witches, not costume-party witches.

## Design language & takeaways for Tend

- **User-built ritual objects are the core mechanic, not a side feature.** Cosmic Wheel proves that letting a user *construct* their own occult artifact — rather than choosing from a menu of pre-built ones — is the single most powerful loop in this space. Tend's offerings should be assembled, not selected.
- **Constrained generativity is the design problem to solve.** A small, symbolically rich component vocabulary (suit + centerpiece + ornaments + frame) yields huge perceived variety while maintaining art-direction coherence. Don't go for unlimited custom; go for constrained-but-meaningful.
- **The construction surface should be diegetic.** Cosmic Wheel's card forge looks like an altar / workbench, not a settings screen. Tend's offering-builder should look like a ritual surface, with components as physical-feeling props.
- **Show the artifact assembling in real time.** Every component choice in Cosmic Wheel updates the card immediately. The player watches their offering take shape. This is non-negotiable for the emotional register.
- **Consecrate the completion moment.** When a card is finished, there's a halo / glow / pause. Tend should mark the moment an offering is finalized with a small ceremonial flourish — it converts a UI action into a ritual act.
- **Use created artifacts in later flows.** Cards Fortuna built are the cards she draws in readings — they have consequences. Tend's offerings should reappear: in deity dialogue, in streak displays, in "consult your altar" moments.
- **Consult-the-cards / consult-the-deities as decision-support framing.** Cosmic Wheel reframes "make a choice" as "draw a card" — divination becomes a UX paradigm. Tend can frame habit-decisions as deity consultations: "ask your patron," not "set a goal."
- **Branching consequences make the practice feel real.** Readings shift Sisterhood politics. Offerings in Tend should accumulate into something visible — relationship with deity, altar state, narrative shifts — not just streak numbers.
- **Manga-influenced pixel/illustration is an underused register.** It's neither cute-pastel nor dark-gothic — it lets deities be adults with histories. This is a strong differentiator from Finch, Fabulous, and Headspace.
- **Muted-rich palette over high-saturation.** Deep purples, faded golds, parchment cream. Resist app-store-friendly saturation — the sincerity of the aesthetic depends on restraint.
- **Diegetic UI ornament over neutral chrome.** Dialogue plates, choice cards, and frames should feel engraved or inscribed, not generic. Every UI surface is an opportunity for occult ornament.
- **Treat deity / character portraits as the primary visual subject.** Cosmic Wheel gives portraits half the frame. Tend's deity moments should commit similarly — a full-presence portrait when the patron speaks, not an avatar icon.
